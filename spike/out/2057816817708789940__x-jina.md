Most people know they need to automate with AI, but they have no clue where to start.

They hear about workflows, agents, and ten different AI tools and feel immediately overwhelmed.

Too many tools, too complicated, and too much friction for getting started.

So, they resort to doing nothing and end up wasting hours per week on manual tasks that AI could've handled if they'd followed the right steps.

In this article, I am going to give you the exact three-step framework I use to identify, design, and build any automation in under 10 minutes - no coding or expensive AI tools required.

This framework applies to any AI automation you want to build for any aspect of your life.

## Step One: What do I automate?

Before you touch a single tool, you need to know what you are actually automating. This might sound obvious, but most people skip this step entirely and end up drowning in options with zero useful automations actually built.

I want you to conduct a time audit exercise: write down every task you do on a daily, weekly, and monthly basis. Do NOT filter anything out at this stage.

The goal is just to get it on paper (or organized digitally); it doesn't matter whether or not you think it can be automated.

Three categories: Daily tasks. Weekly tasks. Monthly tasks.

Tip: actually go through a full day/weekly routine so you can accurately time log your tasks.

Once you have your list, go through it and ask two questions for each item:

1.   Can this be fully automated? 
2.   If not, can AI handle 50-80% of it, so I only handle the final step? 

That second question matters most. A lot of people look at their list and think nothing is automatable because they are thinking only about full automation. But partial automation is just as valuable.

To give you guys a tangible example: I use AI in many ways to help with my writing here on X. However, I do not want AI to write my posts end-to-end. So, I use AI to handle all the research, scraping, grammar checking, and grunt work that goes into hitting the publish button.

The curation and taste are still mine, but I leverage AI systems to automate tasks that don't require human touch.

Pro tip: Use voice prompting to build this list. Open Claude or ChatGPT and explain that you want to map out everything you do daily and weekly in order to automate it. Ask it to prompt you with questions. You will remember things through conversation that you would never think to write down.

## Step Two: Designing the solution

Now, you have your list. What I want you to do now is just pick one thing to start with.

Ideally, this is a high-leverage daily task that, if automated, could save you 30-60 minutes+ per day.

For me, automating my entire content research system (via Slack + Perplexity Computer + OpenClaw) saved me hours per week.

For you, this could be spreadsheet work, email management, calendar management, to-do list tracking, accounting, creative design work, etc.

The biggest mistake people make at this stage is trying to automate everything at once. They look at a list of 20 tasks and start building half-functional workflows for everything.

Don't do that. Instead, pick the highest-leverage item and commit to automating just one workflow per week.

In a perfect world, that means in 2-3 months from now, you'll have 10+ automations running at once.

Once you have your task, use this prompt in Claude (or any LLM) to reverse engineer exactly how to automate the workflow you need.

## Step Three: Execution layer

Two tools handle the majority of automations I run. Both use agent architecture, meaning they do not just answer questions; they execute actual workflows on your behalf.

If you're a complete beginner, just pick one of these tools:

1.   Perplexity Computer 

Since its release, Perplexity Computer has quickly become one of my most-used AI tools. What makes it different from a standard LLM is that it leverages multiple AI models inside a single workflow.

It can use Gemini for research, Claude for synthesis, and GPT for structuring. It automatically selects the best model at each step, so you don't have to manage any of it.

A real example from my own workflow: I use it to build a daily content brief for the AI Edge team. The prompt tells Computer to research developments in AI, identify the strongest ideas, and package them into a ready-to-use brief. It spins up multiple sub-agents running in parallel, synthesizes the outputs, and delivers the brief automatically.

What used to take my team and me hours of manual research now runs automatically every morning.

Perplexity Computer's only downside is its cost. It works extremely well and is genuinely useful, but that productivity comes at a premium. If you don't have the means to invest in a premium tool, resort to the alternative option below.

2. Manus

Manus is the more accessible alternative, and for most workflows, it performs just as well. Same agent architecture (it can research, synthesize, create files, generate images, and automate recurring tasks), but at a lower price point than Computer (with usage limits).

A practical example: give it a prompt to generate a daily intelligence brief, scan for the top developments across your niche, and deliver a concise summary to a log file.
